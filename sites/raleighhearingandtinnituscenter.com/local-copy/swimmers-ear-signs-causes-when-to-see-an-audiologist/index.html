Swimmer’s Ear: Signs, Causes, and When to See a Doctor

Table of Contents
  • Swimmer’s ear is a common outer ear infection often caused by water trapped in the ear canal after swimming.
  • Early symptoms like itchiness, pain, or blockage should not be ignored.
  • Raleigh Hearing & Tinnitus Center connects you to expert audiologists in Raleigh, NC, providing expert hearing care and guidance.

Many people first hear about swimmer’s ear after a long day at the pool, lake, or beach. While swimmers are at higher risk, swimmer’s ear can affect anyone. Warm weather, humidity, and trapped moisture in the ear canal can all play a role.

At Raleigh Hearing & Tinnitus Center, our audiologists help patients recognize swimmer’s ear early, understand why it happens, and know when professional care is needed to protect hearing and comfort.

What Is Swimmer’s Ear?

Swimmer’s ear is an infection of the outer ear canal, which is the passage that carries sound from the outside of the ear to the eardrum. The medical name for swimmer’s ear is otitis externa.

This infection often starts when water stays in the ear canal. The warm, dark space makes it easy for bacteria to grow, especially during hot weather or periods of high humidity.

Why Swimmer’s Ear Happens

The ear canal has natural defenses, including earwax, which helps keep moisture and bacteria out. When these defenses break down, infection can begin.

Common causes include:

  • Water trapped in the ear after swimming in a pool, lake, or ocean
  • Frequent exposure to humidity
  • Scratching the ear canal
  • Using cotton swabs or other objects in the ear
  • Wearing earbuds, hearing aids, or earplugs that trap moisture

Even a small scratch in the ear canal can allow bacteria to enter.

Who Is Most at Risk?

Anyone can get swimmer’s ear, but higher risk groups include:

  • Children and teens who swim often
  • Adults who spend a lot of time in water
  • Swimmers who do not dry their ears fully
  • People living in warm or humid climates
  • Individuals who clean their ears aggressively

You do not need to be a competitive swimmer to develop swimmer’s ear.

Early Symptoms of Swimmer’s Ear

Swimmer’s ear often begins with mild symptoms that can worsen quickly.

Early signs include:

  • Itchiness inside the ear
  • Mild ear pain
  • Redness of the ear canal
  • A feeling of fullness or blockage

At this stage, hearing may still seem normal, but discomfort is often present.

More Advanced Symptoms

If the infection progresses, symptoms may include:

  • Pain when touching or pulling the ear
  • Swelling that narrows the ear canal
  • Drainage from the ear
  • Muffled hearing caused by blockage
  • Pain when chewing or moving the jaw

These symptoms are a strong sign that it is time to see a doctor.

Can Swimmer’s Ear Affect Hearing?

Yes. Swelling and fluid in the ear canal can block sound from reaching the eardrum. This can cause:

  • Temporary hearing loss
  • A plugged or full sensation
  • Distorted or muffled sound

Once the infection clears, hearing usually returns to normal.

Swimmer’s Ear vs. Other Ear Infections

Swimmer’s ear affects the outer ear canal, not the middle ear.

Key differences:

  • Swimmer’s ear pain gets worse when the ear is touched
  • Middle ear infections often cause pressure or fever
  • Swimmer’s ear is more common after water exposure

A proper exam helps identify the correct condition.

What Not to Do

Some home remedies can make swimmer’s ear worse.

Avoid:

  • Using cotton swabs
  • Putting drops in the ear without guidance
  • Using alcohol or peroxide in a painful ear
  • Ignoring pain, swelling, or drainage

These actions can increase irritation and delay healing.

How Swimmer’s Ear Is Diagnosed

A doctor diagnoses swimmer’s ear by examining the ear canal. They look for:

  • Redness and swelling
  • Drainage
  • Tenderness

In some cases, hearing may be checked to rule out other issues.

Treatment for Swimmer’s Ear

Treatment depends on how severe the infection is.

Common treatments include:

  • Prescription ear drops
  • Keeping the ear dry
  • Avoiding swimming during healing
  • Pain relief as recommended

In more serious cases, antibiotics may be prescribed to clear the infection.

Why Seeing a Doctor Matters

Seeing a doctor early can:

  • Reduce pain faster
  • Prevent the infection from spreading
  • Shorten recovery time
  • Protect hearing health

People with diabetes or weakened immune systems should seek care right away.

Preventing Swimmer’s Ear

Prevention focuses on keeping the ear canal dry and protected.

Helpful tips include:

  • Dry ears gently after swimming
  • Tilt your head to drain water
  • Avoid putting objects in the ear
  • Remove wet earbuds or hearing aids
  • Use swim earplugs if recommended

Custom earplugs can help frequent swimmers reduce water exposure safely.

Swimmer’s Ear and Hearing Aids

Hearing aid users should be careful with moisture. Trapped moisture can:

  • Irritate the ear canal
  • Increase bacteria growth
  • Lead to discomfort or infection

Proper cleaning and drying routines are important.

Children and Swimmer’s Ear

Children are more likely to get swimmer’s ear due to:

  • Smaller ear canals
  • Frequent swimming
  • Difficulty describing symptoms

Watch for ear pulling, crying, or trouble sleeping after swimming.

Frequently Asked Questions

Are home remedies effective for swimmer’s ear?

Home remedies may help prevent swimmer’s ear in some cases, but they are not a reliable treatment once an infection has started. Using household items in the ear can sometimes make symptoms worse or delay proper care. If you have pain, drainage, or ongoing itchiness, it’s best to see a doctor for safe and effective treatment.

Is swimmer’s ear contagious?

No, swimmer’s ear is not contagious. It cannot be spread from person to person, even with skin contact. The infection develops when moisture, bacteria, or irritation affect the ear canal, often after swimming or exposure to humidity.

How serious is swimmer’s ear?

Swimmer’s ear is usually mild when treated early, but it can become more serious if ignored. Without proper care, swelling and blockage can increase pain and affect hearing. In rare cases, the infection can spread to nearby tissue. Seeing a doctor early helps prevent complications and speeds healing.

How to relieve swimmer’s ear pain?

Pain relief starts with keeping the ear dry and avoiding pressure or touching the ear. Over-the-counter pain relievers may help, but the most effective relief often comes from prescription ear drops that reduce infection and inflammation. If pain worsens or does not improve within a day or two, a doctor should evaluate the ear.
