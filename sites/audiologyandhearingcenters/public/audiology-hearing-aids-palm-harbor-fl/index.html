Frequently Asked Questions

Can’t find the answers you’re looking for? Contact Us.

Can you self-refer to an audiologist?

Yes, you can go directly to an audiologist for a hearing evaluation and access to hearing aids without needing a referral from a doctor or nurse. This process, known as self-referral, allows you to schedule an appointment on your own. However, while audiologists don’t require a referral for ethical or licensing reasons, many insurance providers and Medicare require one for coverage.

Do you need a doctor’s referral for a hearing test?

Medicare requires a physician referral for any diagnostic hearing tests that will be reimbursed. The main factor affecting coverage is the medical necessity of the test. To qualify for reimbursement, the healthcare provider must document the symptoms, concerns, or medical reasons that justify the need for the hearing evaluation.

Is a hearing aid specialist the same as an audiologist?

No, they have different roles. A hearing aid specialist (HIS) can assess basic hearing needs and fit hearing aids. An audiologist, however, has more extensive training and can diagnose and treat a wider range of hearing and balance issues. Audiologists also focus on identifying the underlying causes of hearing loss, whereas hearing aid specialists primarily conduct hearing tests to fit and program hearing aids.

How do I know if I have hearing loss?

Hearing loss can develop gradually, making it difficult to notice at first. Some common signs include:

  • Asking people to repeat themselves often
  • Struggling to hear conversations in noisy places
  • Turning up the volume on the TV or phone higher than others prefer
  • Feeling like people mumble or speak too softly
  • Difficulty understanding high-pitched voices, such as children or women
  • Ringing or buzzing in the ears (tinnitus)
  • Feeling exhausted from trying to listen


If you experience any of these symptoms, a hearing evaluation with an audiologist can determine whether you have hearing loss and what steps to take next. Early detection and treatment can improve communication and overall quality of life.
