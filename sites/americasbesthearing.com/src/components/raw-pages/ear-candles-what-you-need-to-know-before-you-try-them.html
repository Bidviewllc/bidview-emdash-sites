Ear Candles: What You Need to Know Before You Try Them

  • October 2, 2025
  • Hearing
  • Kylie Kasel, Au.D.
Table of Contents

Many people struggle with earwax buildup and look for simple home remedies to clean their ears. One method that often gets attention is ear candling. Ear candles are hollow cones made of fabric coated in wax, usually beeswax or paraffin. During use, the narrow end of the candle is placed into the ear canal, and the other end is lit on fire.

The idea is that the flame creates a suction effect, pulling out earwax and other debris. It sounds appealing to people who want an easy, natural fix, but the truth is very different. Research shows that ear candles do not remove earwax and can actually be harmful.

Do Ear Candles Really Work?

The short answer is no. Studies have shown that ear candling does not produce suction strong enough to pull earwax out of the ear. The residue often found inside the cone after use is not earwax but ash and burned candle material.

This means that not only does ear candling fail to clean your ears, but it may actually leave behind more debris than you started with.

Risks of Using Ear Candles

While ear candles are marketed as safe and natural, the risks are very real. Some of the most common problems include:

  • Burns – Hot wax or flames near the ear and face can cause serious burns.
  • Blockages – Melted candle wax can drip into the ear canal, creating a blockage that makes hearing worse.
  • Ear damage – Inserting the candle too far can damage the ear canal or eardrum.
  • Infections – Blockages and burns can increase the risk of ear infections.

Many people turn to ear candling because they believe it is a safe alternative, but these risks often outweigh any supposed benefit.

Safer Ways to Manage Earwax

Earwax, also called cerumen, is actually good for your ears. It protects the ear canal, traps dirt, and keeps bacteria from causing infections. For most people, earwax naturally works its way out of the ear on its own. But sometimes it builds up and causes problems like muffled hearing, discomfort, or ringing in the ears.

Instead of using ear candles, here are some safer options:

  • Professional earwax removal – Audiologists and hearing instrument specialists use safe tools and methods to remove wax. We provide professional ear wax removal in Michigan, Minnesota, and Florida.
  • Ear drops – Over-the-counter drops can soften wax, helping it move out on its own.
  • Gentle cleaning – Use a damp cloth to wipe the outer ear only. Never put cotton swabs or other objects inside your ear canal.

When to See a Hearing Professional

You should schedule an appointment if you experience:

  • Sudden or ongoing hearing loss
  • Ear pain or pressure
  • Persistent ringing in the ears (tinnitus)
  • Dizziness or balance problems
  • A history of repeated earwax buildup

Why Ear Health Matters

Good ear health is about more than just comfort. Blocked ears can interfere with hearing aids, making them less effective. Untreated buildup or damage may also impact your ability to connect with family, friends, and the world around you.

That is why it is important to seek help from professionals who understand ear health and hearing care. Our team takes the time to listen to your concerns and recommend safe, effective solutions.

The Bottom Line on Ear Candles

Ear candles may look like a simple answer, but they do not work and can cause more harm than good. Your ears are delicate, and caring for them requires the right knowledge and tools. If you are worried about earwax buildup or any hearing concerns, the safest choice is to see a hearing professional.
