Hyperacusis: Signs and Symptoms

Hyperacusis is more than just a dislike for loud noises. It’s a heightened sensitivity that can make even moderate sounds seem painfully loud. People with hyperacusis often describe everyday noises like clinking dishes, car engines, or a baby crying as physically painful or distressing. This condition may be accompanied by anxiety, irritability, or even social withdrawal due to the discomfort it causes.

Common signs include:

  • Discomfort or pain when hearing normal sounds
  • Increased sensitivity to high-pitched or sudden noises
  • Avoidance of noisy environments
  • Trouble concentrating due to sound distractions
  • Ear fullness or ringing (tinnitus)

Hyperacusis: Causes

There are several potential causes of hyperacusis. Often, it results from damage to the inner ear or hearing system due to:

  • Noise exposure (loud music, machinery, or explosions)
  • Head injuries or concussions
  • Ear infections or ear surgeries
  • Neurological conditions like migraines or Lyme disease
  • Medication side effects
  • Chronic stress or anxiety

In some cases, hyperacusis may develop gradually, while in others it appears suddenly. Regardless of how it starts, the impact on daily life can be frustrating and emotionally draining. Identifying the root cause is the first step toward managing your symptoms and reclaiming comfort in your everyday environment.

Hyperacusis Treatment

Treating hyperacusis often involves a combination of sound therapy, counseling, and lifestyle changes. One common approach is desensitization therapy, where the ears are gradually retrained to tolerate normal sound levels. This can be done using low-level noise generators or environmental sounds. Cognitive behavioral therapy (CBT) is also helpful for managing the emotional response to sound sensitivity.

In some cases, hearing aids with sound-masking features can provide relief, especially if tinnitus is also present. Hyperacusis Home Remedies

While professional treatment is always recommended, there are home remedies and lifestyle changes that may help reduce the impact of hyperacusis:

  • Create a calming environment: Avoid overly quiet spaces, which can make sound sensitivity worse. Gentle background noise (like soft music or a white noise machine) can help ease the discomfort.
  • Wear sound-filtering earplugs: If you’re in a noisy area, these earplugs can reduce intensity without blocking sound completely. Avoid using foam earplugs all the time, as overprotection can make hyperacusis worse.
  • Practice relaxation techniques: Stress and anxiety can heighten sensitivity. Try mindfulness, deep breathing, or meditation to calm your nervous system.
  • Limit loud sound exposure: Avoid concerts, loud machinery, or other high-volume environments, especially without hearing protection.
  • Keep a sound journal: Track which sounds trigger discomfort and share this information with your audiologist.

Remember, while these methods may provide temporary relief, they do not replace a full evaluation by a qualified professional. 

Hyperacusis Risk Factors

Anyone can develop hyperacusis, but certain risk factors increase the likelihood:

  • Frequent exposure to loud noise, such as musicians or construction workers
  • A history of ear infections or trauma
  • Neurological conditions, including migraines or epilepsy
  • Mental health conditions, such as anxiety or PTSD
  • Use of ototoxic medications that can damage hearing structures
  • Tinnitus or hearing loss may also be linked to hyperacusis

Understanding these risk factors can help with early detection and management. If you fall into one of these categories and notice increased sound sensitivity, it’s best to schedule a hearing evaluation with an audiologist.
