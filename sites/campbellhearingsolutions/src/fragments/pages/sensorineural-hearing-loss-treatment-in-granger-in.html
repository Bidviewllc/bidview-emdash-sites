Sensorineural Hearing Loss (SNHL) happens when the inner ear or the nerve that sends sound signals to the brain is damaged. This type of hearing loss makes it hard to hear soft sounds and can make louder sounds unclear or distorted. SNHL is usually permanent because the tiny hair cells in the inner ear do not grow back once they are damaged.

Why Seeking Treatment Is Important

If left untreated, SNHL can make it harder to understand conversations, enjoy daily activities, and stay connected with family and friends. It can also lead to social isolation, frustration, and even memory problems over time. The good news is that with the right treatment, you can improve your hearing and quality of life.

Symptoms of Sensorineural Hearing Loss

SNHL can develop gradually or suddenly. Some common signs include:

  • Difficulty hearing soft or whispered sounds
  • Trouble understanding speech – people may sound like they are mumbling
  • Frequently asking others to repeat themselves
  • Struggling to hear in noisy places like restaurants or group settings
  • Ringing in the ears (tinnitus)
  • Hearing better in one ear than the other
  • Feeling like sounds are muffled or far away
  • Difficulty hearing high-pitched sounds like children’s voices or birds

If you experience any of these symptoms, it’s important to schedule a hearing test with an audiologist. Early diagnosis can help you get the best treatment for your hearing needs.

Causes of Sensorineural Hearing Loss

SNHL can be caused by different factors, including:

  • Aging (Presbycusis) – Over time, the tiny hair cells in the inner ear wear down and stop working properly
  • Loud Noise Exposure – Repeated exposure to loud sounds like concerts, power tools, or gunfire can damage the inner ear
  • Genetics – Some people are born with SNHL or have a family history of hearing loss
  • Certain Medications – Some drugs, known as ototoxic medications, can damage the inner ear and cause hearing loss
  • Head Injuries – A strong blow to the head can harm the inner ear or the hearing nerve
  • Illnesses and Infections – Viruses, fevers, and autoimmune diseases can sometimes cause permanent hearing loss

Knowing the cause of your SNHL can help your audiologist recommend the most effective treatment for your specific needs.

Sensorineural Hearing Loss Diagnosis and Treatment

At Campbell Hearing Solutions, diagnosing SNHL starts with a hearing test performed by an audiologist. This test measures how well you hear different sounds and speech. If hearing loss is found, our Granger, IN audiologists will discuss the best treatment options to help you hear better.

Treatment Options for SNHL

  • Hearing Aids – These small devices make sounds louder and clearer. Many modern hearing aids include features like noise reduction, Bluetooth connectivity, and rechargeable batteries
  • Cochlear Implants – For those with severe hearing loss who don’t benefit from hearing aids, a cochlear implant may be recommended. This device sends sound signals directly to the hearing nerve
  • Bone-Anchored Hearing Aids (BAHA) – This device attaches to the bone behind the ear and helps people with single-sided deafness or mixed hearing loss

Your audiologist will help choose the best treatment based on your hearing loss and lifestyle needs.

What Is the Best Treatment for Sensorineural Hearing Loss?

The best treatment depends on how much hearing loss you have:

  • Mild to Moderate SNHL – Hearing aids are often the best choice
  • Severe SNHL – If hearing aids aren’t enough, cochlear implants may help
  • Single-Sided Deafness or Mixed Hearing Loss – A bone-anchored hearing aid (BAHA) may be the right solution

Our Granger, IN audiologists will work with you to find the hearing solution that fits you best.

Can You Fix Sensorineural Hearing Loss?

Right now, there is no cure for SNHL because the tiny hair cells in the inner ear do not grow back. But hearing aids and cochlear implants can help you hear better and improve your daily life. Getting treatment early gives your brain more time to adjust to hearing devices.

How Do You Prevent Further Sensorineural Hearing Loss?

While you can’t always prevent SNHL, you can take steps to protect your hearing:

  • Avoid loud noises – Wear hearing protection at concerts, while using power tools, or around gunfire
  • Turn down the volume – Keep music and TV at a safe level
  • Take listening breaks – Give your ears rest if you use headphones often
  • Check your medications – Ask your doctor if any of your medications could affect your hearing
  • Get regular hearing tests – Early detection helps slow hearing loss and improve treatment success

Protecting your hearing now can help you enjoy better hearing in the future.
