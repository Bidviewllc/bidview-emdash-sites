How do I know if I have hearing loss?

Hearing loss can develop slowly, making it hard to notice at first. Common signs include:

  • Frequently asking people to repeat themselves
  • Struggling to hear conversations in noisy places
  • Turning up the volume on the TV or phone higher than others prefer
  • Feeling like others are mumbling or speaking too softly
  • Difficulty understanding high-pitched voices, such as children or women
  • Ringing or buzzing in the ears (tinnitus)
  • Feeling exhausted from trying to listen

If any of these sound familiar, it’s a good idea to get a hearing test. An audiologist can check your hearing and help you find the right solution. Catching hearing loss early can make a big difference in your daily life and communication.

Can you self-refer to an audiologist?

Yes! You can make an appointment with an audiologist on your own—no referral from a doctor or nurse is needed. This is called self-referral and makes it easy to get the hearing care you need. Just keep in mind that some insurance plans, including Medicare, may require a referral for coverage.

How is hearing loss diagnosed?

Hearing loss is diagnosed through a complete hearing evaluation done by an audiologist or hearing instrument specialist. This includes a series of tests to measure how well you hear and check for any related issues. After the testing, the audiologist will review your results, explain the type and degree of hearing loss (if any), and recommend next steps, like hearing aids, medical care, or other solutions.

What type of hearing aid do I need?

The right hearing aid depends on your hearing needs, lifestyle, and preferences. Hearing aids come in different styles and offer various features. Your audiologist will help you choose the best option based on your hearing level, how active you are, how comfortable you are with small devices, and what look you prefer.
