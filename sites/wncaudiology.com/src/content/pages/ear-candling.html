Ear Candles: Safety, Risks, and Better Alternatives

  • February 4, 2026

What Are Ear Candles?

Ear candles (also called ear coning) are hollow tubes made of cloth and wax. One end is placed in the ear while the other end is lit on fire. The claim is that heat creates suction that pulls earwax (cerumen) out of the ear canal.

This sounds simple, but the science does not support it.

Do Ear Candles Actually Work?

No. Ear candles do not work.

Clinical testing shows that ear candles:

  • Do not create negative pressure (vacuum)
  • Do not remove wax from deep in the ear canal
  • Leave behind candle residue that looks like earwax

The “Ash Myth” Explained

Many people believe the dark material inside a used candle is earwax. In reality:

  • The material is burned candle wax and cloth
  • The same residue appears even when candles are burned without an ear
  • No wax is pulled past the eardrum due to anatomy and air pressure

From an audiology standpoint, earwax cannot move outward against gravity, anatomy, and pressure from heat.

If earwax is causing trouble or affecting your hearing, you may need to see an audiologist for a professional evaluation or hearing test.

Common Ear Candle Injuries We See

As hearing care professionals in Western North Carolina, we regularly see patients harmed by ear candling.

Documented Injuries Include:

  • Burns to the ear canal and outer ear
  • Wax drips sealing the canal shut
  • Perforated (torn) eardrums
  • Ear infections
  • Hearing loss
  • Balance problems

These injuries often require medical diagnosis, treatment, and follow-up audiology assessment.

Earwax Blockage Symptoms

Many people try ear candles because of symptoms caused by earwax blockage, including:

  • Muffled hearing
  • Ear pressure or fullness
  • Ringing in the ears (tinnitus)
  • Dizziness
  • Hearing aid feedback
  • Pain or discomfort

These symptoms are not signs that you need ear candles. They are signs you need a proper ear assessment.

Do Ear Candles Work Better Than Over-the-Counter Drops?

No. Ear candles are less effective and far more dangerous than drops.

Over-the-Counter Drops:

  • Soften earwax
  • May help mild blockages near the outer ear
  • Should only be used when approved by a clinician

Ear Candles:

  • Add heat and fire to a sensitive area
  • Push wax deeper into the canal
  • Increase risk of burns and infection

If drops don’t help within a few days, professional care is needed.

Are Ear Candles Safe for Seniors With Hearing Aids?

Absolutely not.

Seniors who wear hearing aids face higher risks because:

  • Hearing aids increase cerumen buildup
  • Skin inside the ear becomes thinner with age
  • Balance systems are more sensitive
  • Hearing thresholds may already be reduced

Burns or wax blockage can:

  • Damage hearing aids
  • Worsen hearing loss
  • Delay proper diagnosis

At WNC Audiology, we strongly advise seniors never to use ear candles.

Ear Candles vs. Professional Microsuction: A Safety Comparison

Why Microsuction Is the Safest Option

Microsuction uses gentle suction under direct visual guidance. This allows the clinician to:

  • See the ear canal clearly
  • Avoid the eardrum
  • Remove wax precisely
  • Protect hearing thresholds and balance

No heat. No water. No fire.

What Should I Do If I Have Earwax Stuck Deep in My Canal?

Do not try to remove it yourself.

Instead:

  • Schedule a professional assessment
  • Avoid cotton swabs, candles, or tools
  • Keep the ear dry and protected

Deep wax requires clinical tools and proper diagnosis to prevent injury.

Can Ear Candles Cause Permanent Eardrum Damage?

Yes.

Eardrum perforations from hot wax or pressure changes can lead to:

  • Permanent hearing loss
  • Chronic infections
  • Long-term balance problems

Some damage cannot be reversed. Early diagnosis is critical.

How Long Does a Professional Ear Cleaning Appointment Take?

Most appointments take:

  • 20–30 minutes
  • Including ear examination and cleaning
  • Same-day relief for most patients

Safe Ear Hygiene Tips (Clinically Approved)

  • Let earwax do its job, it protects the ear.
  • Clean only the outer ear.
  • Use drops only if advised.
  • Schedule routine ear checks if you wear hearing aids.
  • Seek professional help for blockage symptoms.

Avoid:

  • Candles
  • Cotton swabs
  • Metal tools
  • Home suction devices

Why Ear Candles Are Still Popular (And Why That’s a Problem)

Ear candles are often marketed as:

  • “Natural”
  • “Ancient”
  • “Holistic”

But natural does not mean safe.

There is no medical evidence supporting ear candling, and many professional organizations advise against it.

When Earwax Is Actually a Problem

Earwax becomes a problem when it:

  • Blocks sound
  • Traps bacteria
  • Interferes with hearing aids
  • Affects balance or comfort

Only a trained professional can make this diagnosis accurately.
